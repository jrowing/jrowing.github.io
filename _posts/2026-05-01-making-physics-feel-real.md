Teaching physics in the Seychelles offers something most classrooms can't: the ocean is always right there.

Wave optics becomes a snorkelling trip. Refraction is something you *see* every time you look down through clear water at a fish that isn't quite where it appears to be. The atmosphere's lapse rate is visible in the way clouds form over the granite peaks of Praslin each afternoon like clockwork.

Twenty years of classrooms have convinced me that the gap between abstract physics and genuine understanding closes fastest when students encounter the phenomenon first and the equation second.

## What I've been trying

This term I've been running practicals backwards — observation first, model second. Students collect data before they've seen the formula. They have to make sense of a pattern before I hand them the language for it.

The results have been surprising. Students who struggle with algebra first are often the sharpest observers. Giving them the observational work first means they arrive at the equation with something to attach it to.
