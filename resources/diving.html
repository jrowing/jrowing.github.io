Moving to the Seychelles opened up an entirely new world below the waterline. The Indian Ocean around these islands is extraordinary — ancient granite boulders draped in coral, sharks patrolling the blue, and a warmth and clarity that makes every dive feel like flying.

Favourite dive sites

Three dives that stay with you

The Seychelles has over 50 named dive sites across the inner islands. These three have stuck with me — for very different reasons.

Site 01 · Mahé · Advanced

The Ennerdale

My first wreck — and the most dramatic dive in the Indian Ocean

📏 30–35m ⚓ Wreck ⏱ ~30 min bottom time 🎓 Deep Diver / AOW required

On 1 June 1970, the RFA Ennerdale — a 215-metre British Royal Fleet Auxiliary tanker — struck an uncharted pinnacle off the north-east coast of Mahé. With no way to pump the oil before she sank and weather conditions closing in, the decision was made to blow her open. Wessex helicopters attached explosive charges to the hull; speedboats plucked the divers clear with seconds to spare. She was destroyed deliberately to save the islands.

Now she lies in two enormous sections at around 30 metres, the largest shipwreck in the Seychelles. The stern is relatively intact — vast, eerie and covered in life. Giant groupers drift through the engine room. Schools of fusiliers twist through the superstructure. Eagle rays pass overhead. She is, without question, the most poignant dive I have ever done: a fine ship reduced to rubble, and in doing so, she became something extraordinary.

At 30+ metres you get limited bottom time — around 20–30 minutes — so it repays multiple visits. Take a mandatory safety stop at 5 metres, and if you're lucky, dolphins will join you on the way up.

Giant Groupers Eagle Rays Giant Moray Eels Scorpion Fish Schools of Fusiliers Barracuda Lion Fish Sharks Dolphins (safety stop!)

Site 02 · Praslin / La Digue · Advanced

South Marianne

Granite cathedrals, ripping current, and grey reef sharks in every direction

📏 19–25m avg 🪨 Granite pinnacles 💨 Strong current — drift dive 🚤 40 min from Praslin

South Marianne is due east of La Digue, close enough to the open ocean to feel the difference the moment you descend. You make a negative entry straight into the current, dropping fast into a maze of granite pinnacles that reach from the seafloor to the island walls above. The boulders are enormous — house-sized — covered in swaying soft coral. Between them run sand channels and canyons, narrow enough in places to feel almost enclosed.

The ocean moves through this place. You can either hunker down behind a boulder and watch the pelagics cruise past in the blue, or let the current take you on a fast drift through the gullies. Grey reef sharks are regulars here — not the occasional glimpse you get at shallower sites, but proper encounters. They patrol the walls with total indifference. Eagle rays, barracuda, and schools of jack tumble past in the current. Giant moray eels peer from the crevices. The boulders creak and groan in the surge.

This is the most demanding site accessible from Praslin, and the most rewarding. Excellent visibility year-round. Not suitable for beginners — the current is serious.

Grey Reef Sharks Eagle Rays Hawksbill Turtles Guitarfish Giant Moray Eels Schooling Jacks Barracuda Napoleon Wrasse Whitetip Reef Sharks Batfish

Site 03 · Praslin · All levels

Booby Rock

A rock in the ocean that punches wildly above its weight

📏 15–25m 🦈 Sharks year-round 🐟 Outstanding fish life 🚤 30–35 min from Praslin

Booby Rock barely exists above the surface — it's named after the boobies that roost on it, and from the boat it looks like nothing much at all. Beneath the surface is a different story entirely. The sloping granite walls of this little island are covered in crevices and overhangs, every one of them sheltering something — lobster, octopus, scorpion fish, moray eels — while above, the open water teems with pelagics.

This site is renowned for its sharks. At depth you can routinely see up to fifteen grey reef sharks at once, patrolling the walls in loose formation. Nurse sharks rest in the deeper ledges. Whitetip reef sharks are almost always present. On a good day you'll also see yellowfin tuna, amberjacks and stingrays in the blue, with bumphead parrotfish foraging along the reef and turtles drifting overhead. Dolphins have been seen here too.

Good visibility and manageable depth make this a wonderful dive for a wide range of experience levels, and there's enough biodiversity to keep you very busy. One of the best dives on Praslin — full stop.

Grey Reef Sharks (up to 15) Nurse Sharks Whitetip Reef Sharks Turtles Stingrays Yellowfin Tuna Bumphead Parrotfish Amberjacks Eagle Rays Lobsters Octopus Dolphins
