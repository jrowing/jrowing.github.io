60+ Demonstrations

Hands-on physics demonstrations using everyday objects — designed to spark curiosity and make abstract concepts tangible. Collected over twenty years in classrooms.

I highly recommend the work of David Featonby, who often has a piece on one of these demos at the end of Physics Education. More can be found in Keith Gibbs' The Resourceful Physics Teacher — a book that ought to be required reading on any teacher training programme.

Also worth bookmarking: Caltech Physics Demonstration Archive ↗

69 demonstrations

01 Two Martini Glasses Density
Fill two martini glasses halfway — they look ¾ full. Empty one into the other and it fills exactly to the brim.
Why: A surprising lesson in how vessel shape affects perceived volume.
02 Filling or Spilling Bottles Density
A selection of bottles and beakers — fill one, predict whether the liquid will fill or spill the others.
Why: Leads into discussion of packaging and how bottle shapes create misleading impressions of volume.
03 Floating Orange Density
Float an orange in water. Peel it. It sinks — the aerated peel reduces the orange's average density.
04 Floating Various Fruits Density
Apples float. Lemons float (thick pith). Limes sink (thin skin, little pith). Same principle, different results.
05 Floating Egg in Saltwater Density
An egg sinks in fresh water but floats once salt is dissolved. Compare with the Dead Sea or Blue Lagoon, Iceland.
06 Coke vs. Diet Coke Density
Unopened cans in a tank of water: Diet Coke floats higher. The sugar in regular Coke significantly increases density.
07 Cartesian Diver Density
A sauce packet balanced with paperclips just floats in a sealed 2L bottle. Squeeze the bottle — it sinks.
Why: Squeezing compresses the air pocket in the packet, increasing its density.
08 Dancing Sultanas Density
Drop sultanas into cheap fizzy lemonade. They sink, accumulate bubbles, rise, pop at the surface, and repeat.
09 Grapes in Lemonade Density
Peeled grape stays at the bottom; unpeeled grape rises and falls — the skin provides nucleation sites for bubbles.
10 Burning Tea Bag Thermal
Empty a cylindrical teabag, stand it upright, light it. It burns down — then the ash floats up on the convection current.
11 Three Candles Thermal
Three candles of different heights under an inverted jar. Which goes out first? The tallest — hot CO₂ is less dense than air.
12 Inertia with a Toy Truck Forces
Egg on a toy truck, truck stopped suddenly. The egg keeps moving — Newton's first law made very vivid.
13 Spinning Eggs Forces
Stop a spinning raw egg and release — it restarts. Stop a hard-boiled one — it stays stopped.
Why: The fluid inside the raw egg continues rotating and drags on the shell.
14 Tablecloth Trick Forces
Pull a cloth sharply from under crockery. Objects stay put if the cloth is pulled quickly — inertia in action.
15 Rope on a Cylinder Forces
A few turns of string around a rolling pin holds a 1kg mass without slipping when the pin is lifted.
16 The Falling Cup Forces
A cup on a string over a pencil, a cork on the other end. Released, the cup stops before hitting the ground.
Why: Conservation of angular momentum — the cork speeds up as the string shortens and wraps around the pencil, creating enough friction to arrest the fall.
17 Ball Drop Test Forces
Table tennis ball vs. golf ball dropped together — both land simultaneously, unless dropped from high enough for terminal velocity to differ.
18 Coin and Paper Drop Forces
Drop separately (coin wins), edge-on together (simultaneous), coin on paper (coin slips free). Air resistance demonstrated three ways.
19 Water Jet in Free Fall Fluids
A bottle with a hole jets water — throw it into the air and the jet stops. Bottle and water fall together; weightlessness analogue.
20 Straw in a Sealed Bottle Fluids
Blow air into a sealed bottle to raise water in a straw. Jump off a chair holding the bottle — water shoots out.
Why: In free fall, the air pressure no longer has to support the water's weight.
21 Groan Tube Drop Forces
Drop a groan tube — it goes silent in free fall, then groans again when caught. A nice analogue of weightlessness in orbit.
22 Slinky Spring Drop Forces
Hold a slinky and drop it. The bottom remains stationary until the top arrives — the centre of mass falls at g while the ends are drawn inward.
23 Tennis Ball on a Basketball Forces
Drop a tennis ball balanced on a basketball — the tennis ball shoots up dramatically. Great Y12 momentum analysis (see also: Astroblaster).
Why: The basketball bounces and meets the still-falling tennis ball, transferring energy at twice the relative velocity.
24 Two Metre Rules with Weights Forces
One rule weighted at the top, another at the centre — dropped simultaneously. The centre-weighted one lands first.
25 Weighted vs. Unweighted Rules Forces
A rod with a weight at the centre vs. one with no weight — they land at the same time. Equal acceleration regardless of mass.
26 Broom Handle on Fingers Forces
Rest a broom on two fingers, slide them together. They meet at the centre of mass — friction is tied to the normal reaction force.
27 Shifting Centre of Mass Forces
Repeat #26 with a weight tied to one end. Fingers now meet at the new, shifted centre of mass.
28 Egg Thrown at a Sheet Forces
Throw a raw egg hard at a vertical hung sheet — it doesn't break. The deceleration is too gentle to crack the shell.
29 Spinning Cream Egg Forces
Spin a Cadbury's Cream Egg fast horizontally — it rises up onto one end, like a tippe top. See IOP analysis ↗
30 Newspaper and Thin Wood Forces
A thin strip of wood under newspaper on a table, end protruding. Strike sharply — the wood snaps. F = Δmv/t in action.
31 Blowing Between Paper Strips Fluids
Hold two paper strips and blow between them — they move together. Faster air = lower pressure (Bernoulli).
32 Newton's Cradle Variations Forces
What if metal is placed between stationary balls? What if one end is struck by a hammer heavier than all the balls combined?
33 Newton's Cradle — Bowling Balls Forces
Roll an 8lb ball into three 6lb ones — one 6lb ball moves away. Vary the masses and observe conservation of momentum at scale.
34 Waddling Animal on a Slope Forces
Add mass to a waddling toy animal. Speed up or slow it down depending on placement — centre of mass and stability.
35 Wine Glass and Cork Fluids
Half a cork inside a wine glass. Blow sharply over the top — the cork jumps out. Bernoulli / Venturi effect.
36 Soap-Powered Boat Fluids
Shape a lolly stick into a hull. Drop soap powder on the stern — it chugs forward. Soap reduces surface tension at the rear.
37 Cocktail Sticks and Soap Fluids
Sticks arranged as radii on water — touch centre with paper (they move in), then soap (they spring outward).
38 Knees Against a Wall Forces
Stand with heels against a wall and try to bend down. You fall forward — centre of gravity moves outside the base of support.
39 Breaking a Suspended Tin Forces
Tin of beans on two threads. Pull the lower sharply — it breaks. Pull slowly — the upper breaks. F = Δmv/t demonstrated neatly.
40 Candle at Both Ends Forces
Needle through the centre of a candle, both ends shaved and lit. It rocks like a seesaw — a lovely moments demonstration.
41 Mirror Writing Optics
Write TOM, DICK, HARRY under a mirror. Only DICK appears unaltered — a trick of bilateral symmetry. See IFLScience ↗
42 The Big Circuit EM
Wires running to the back of the room and back. Does the bulb come on immediately? Yes — leads into high-frequency trading and signal latency.
43 Whacking a Suspended Broom Forces
Broom handle suspended on two thin threads, struck with another broom. Hit hard enough — the handle breaks, threads survive. ⚠️ Beware fragments.
44 Ice on Different Surfaces Thermal
Identical ice blocks on a plastic box (cold to touch) and a metal pan (also cold to touch). The metal one melts within a minute — thermal conductivity.
45 Magnet on Japanese Yen EM
Place a neodymium magnet on yen coins. Lift quickly — the coins come with it. A striking Lenz's law demonstration.
46 Suspended Yen Near Magnet EM
A yen coin on a thread — move a neodymium magnet toward it and the coin retreats. Lenz's law again.
47 Floating a Yen on Water Fluids
Float a yen on still water using surface tension. Add drops until it sinks. Add soap — it sinks immediately.
48 Two Test Tubes Density
Small test tube floating inside a larger water-filled one. Invert them — the small tube rises.
49 Glass Rod vs. Foil Rod Near Magnet EM
Suspended glass rod orients at right angles to the magnetic field. Foil rod aligns along the field lines. Diamagnetism vs. paramagnetism.
50 Magnet Rolling on a Slope EM
Roll a small cylindrical magnet down a gentle slope in the N–S direction. It follows a curved arc — not a straight line.
51 Eclipse of Mars Afterimage Optics
Stare at a red disc for 20+ seconds. Look away — a cyan afterimage appears. Opponent process theory in the retina.
52 Skewers Through a Water Bag Fluids
Push a skewer through a water-filled plastic bag — it self-seals and doesn't leak. Works dramatically over someone's head.
53 Elastic Band Temperature Thermal
Stretch an elastic band against your lip — it warms. Let it retract — it cools. Entropy and molecular structure made tactile.
54 Two Balloons Connected Fluids
A large and a small balloon connected. Open the valve — the small one inflates the large one. Counterintuitive surface tension physics.
Why: Pressure inside a balloon depends on the ratio of surface tension to radius — a smaller balloon has higher internal pressure.
55 Inflating Two Balloons Together Fluids
Connect two balloons to a T-piece, try to inflate simultaneously. One always inflates more — same principle as #54.
56 Floating Beaker in a Bottle Density
Float a beaker with a weight in a large bottle of water. Remove the weight into the bottle — the water level falls.
57 Sealed Jar From a Boat Density
Float a boat with a sealed jar. Remove the jar and float it separately — no change in water level. Archimedes carefully examined.
58 Paper Clip Near a Magnet EM
Lower a paper clip on a thread toward a strong magnet — it spins one way going down, the other coming up.
59 Measuring Cylinders of Different Diameters Density
Pour a volume from the wider cylinder into one with half the diameter. The liquid stands four times higher — ratio and fractions made concrete.
60 Balancing Tin Cans on a Metre Rule Density
Two cans balance a rule horizontally. Lower your finger into one can of water — the balance tips. Buoyancy and reaction forces.
61 Dropping Paper and Book Forces
Drop separately, together edge-on, then paper below the book, then paper on top. Three different outcomes — air resistance and relative motion.
62 Ball in a Whirled Bucket Forces
A clear bucket part-filled with water, ball floating on the surface. Whirl in a vertical circle — does the ball stay on the surface?
63 Floating Orange on Scales Density
Balance a beaker of water with an orange beside it. Place the orange in the water — what happens to the scale reading?
64 Elastic Collision — Bowling Balls Forces
Roll one bowling ball into another on rails. The collision is nearly elastic — the first starts rotating and follows the second.
65 Magnet Falling in Copper Pipe EM
Magnet falls slowly through copper pipe; wooden dowel drops normally. Eddy currents induced in the copper oppose the motion.
66 Candle in Water Thermal
Candle in a beaker, water filled to just below the wick. It burns all the way down — the wax wall keeps the water out.
67 Vanishing Test Tubes Optics
Small test tube submerged in baby oil — it vanishes. Refractive indices match. Works as a magic trick: "shatter" and "restore" a test tube.
68 Balancing a Coin on a Banknote Forces
Challenge: balance a coin on the edge of a flat note. Gently bow the note, balance the coin, then carefully straighten — it stays. Centre of mass trick.
69 The Free-Fall Paradox Forces
A ball and cup on a stick, arranged so that when the apparatus is released, the ball lands in the cup every time.
